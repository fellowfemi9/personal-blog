Hey Pregnant Mama 👋

This is an easy reference for pregnancy, your hospital stay, and the first few months with your baby, covering all the possible essentials. I’ve also added my birthing story for a personal touch.
You got this!! 💕

Little Pregnancy Reminder Checklist
☐ Avoid unpasteurized eggs, dairy, and cheese
☐ Skip raw sprouts or undercooked food (well-cooked sprouts are fine)
☐ Avoid ibuprofen or aspirin; follow your doctor’s safe medication list
☐ Avoid skincare products with retinol, benzoyl peroxide, hydroquinone, or salicylic acid
☐ Use mineral sunscreen instead of chemical sunscreen
☐ Avoid laser treatments during pregnancy
☐ Choose products free of parabens, phthalates, and sulfates
☐ Limit prolonged exposure to essential oils
☐ Hold off on raspberry or peppermint herbal tea until late pregnancy (they may stimulate labor)
☐ Avoid lead-based paints (water-based paints are safe for artwork)
☐ Consume baked goods with vanilla extract in moderation (contains small amounts of alcohol)
☐ Use soy-based nail polish remover; formaldehyde-free polish is safe
☐ Avoid pedicure massages in early pregnancy (pressure on ankles can stimulate labor)

Postpartum essentials for mom for the first few weeks

☐ Sanitary pads – Needed for about 4-6 weeks; soft cotton pads are best.
☐ Mesh or stretch panties – Comfortable for recovery; use hospital-provided ones initially.
☐ Sheets for bed – Protects bedding from postpartum bleeding.(You can get some from the hospital)
☐ Postpartum waistband – Supports abdomen and helps with healing of abdominal muscles. Wear continuously for the first 40 days. 
☐ Peri bottle for cleaning – Helps keep the area clean, especially if you have stitches.
☐ Healthy snacks – Quick, nutritious food for night-time nursing like nuts or nutri bar. Fennel seeds boiled in water helps with lactation. 


Nursing - Breastfeeding and pumping 
☐ Breast pump – A double electric pump is ideal. Most insurance plans cover it. Check with your lactation consultant and choose the correct flange size for your nipple. I used Medela double electric pump 
☐ Hands-free pumping bra – Available on Amazon. A nursing bra can be used as well if you aren’t moving around while pumping. Carry a battery for the pump in case you are traveling. 
☐ Breastmilk storage bottles – Useful for storing milk when returning to work. Dr. Browns bottles are great for pumping and storing in the refrigerator. 
☐ Breastmilk storage bags – Optional, for extra storage if you run out of bottles. Some insurance gives this for free along with the pump 
☐ Formula - Good to have as backup for emergencies. Hospitals will provide Similac, Enfamil etc. Bobbie organic formula is a great alternative to breast milk. 
☐ Nipple cream – Soothes and protects nipples. Hospital provides lanolin cream. Earth mama organic butter is helpful as well.  This can even be applied on breast pump flanges before pumping. 
☐ Bottle sterilizer – Very useful for keeping baby bottles and pacifiers clean . Papablic sterilizer set from Amazon works well. Get BPA free bottles along with it. Buy bottle brush and dishwashing liquid that is mild for cleaning in hot water before sterilizing. 
☐ Nursing pillow – Very important to have comfortable pillow that is not too soft. Hospital pillow is firm enough to hold the baby and support your elbows. Get a rocking chair and a step stool for good alignment as moms spend hours nursing during the first few months. Neck and back pain is common and can be avoided with good posture and frequent stretching.

☐ Baby wipes – Gentle, chemical-free ones like pampers sensitive wipes are good. You can take some from the hospital. 
☐ Changing pad and liner – Makes diaper changes easier and cleaner.

☐ Diaper pail or Diaper Genie – Helps contain odors and keeps nursery clean.
☐ Laundry bag & hypoallergenic detergent – Gentle on newborn skin.
☐ Swaddle blankets – For safe and cozy wrapping. There are sleep sacks available for easy wrap around. 
☐ Muslin burp cloths – Keep plenty for feeding and spit-ups.
☐ Hooded towels – Soft towels to keep baby warm after baths.
☐ Baby clothes – Onesies, footed pajamas, and beanies.
☐ White noise machine – Helps soothe baby to sleep.
☐ Rubber sheet liners – Protect mattress from diaper accidents.

☐ Grooming kit – Nail cutter, filer, nasal aspirator, nose/ear wax cleaner (Fridababy products are recommended).

☐ Suction syringe – Provided by the hospital; useful for clearing baby’s nose if congested.

Things to do before the baby comes

☐ Research and choose a pediatrician (preferably close to home)
☐ Take childbirth classes offered at the hospital
☐ Do a hospital tour and learn about the labor ward and parking situation
☐ Reach out to friends for hand-me-downs (babies outgrow things quickly)
☐ Figure out which parent’s insurance policy is best and plan to add baby as a dependent (baby will be added to mom’s plan for the first 30 days)
☐ Order a breast pump (usually covered by insurance) before baby arrives
☐ Set up basic baby essentials like a mini crib or bassinet
☐ Do a car seat inspection at the local highway patrol (often free)
☐ Set up the car seat in the car before bringing baby home
☐ Pack your hospital bag about 3 weeks in advance to avoid last-minute scrambling
☐ Carry a stroller if you don’t want to carry the seat for long distances
☐ Use hospital baby supplies (diapers, wipes, swaddles) and bring extras home to get started
☐ After birth, apply for baby’s Social Security number and birth certificate (usually done at the hospital)

My birthing story 

In the quiet early hours of July 4th, at around 1:30 AM, I noticed my water had broken. A sudden rush of anticipation filled the room—it was finally time. After gathering our things and making sure everything was ready, we made our way to the hospital, arriving around 7:00 AM.

By 8:00 AM, I was admitted to the labor ward, and the nurses began monitoring my vitals and the baby’s heartbeat. Everything looked good, and we began preparing for what would be a long, beautiful journey toward meeting our baby.

To help kickstart labor, the medical team administered the first dose of Cytotec at 8:30 AM. I had breakfast around 9:30 AM, trying to stay energized for the hours ahead. Contractions were mild at first but gradually began to pick up. Another dose of Cytotec was given at 1:30 PM, followed by lunch around 2:45 PM. A third dose was administered at 4:30 PM as my body continued preparing for labor.

By 7:00 PM, the contractions had become much more intense, and I began to feel nauseated as my body worked harder. The discomfort built up, and by 9:00 PM, I decided it was time for the epidural. Relief washed over me soon after, allowing me to rest and refocus.

By 11:30 PM, the moment had come—I was 10 cm dilated and ready to bring my baby into the world. I started pushing around 11:45 PM. The hours that followed were some of the most physically demanding and emotionally charged I’ve ever experienced. With each breath, push, and word of encouragement, I inched closer to holding my little one.

Finally, at 2:37 AM on July 5th, my baby arrived. The room filled with the sound of that first cry, and my heart filled with a love I’d never known before.
Pumping techniques
For exclusive pumping moms:
Required:
1. Double electric pump for 30 mins per session. Single electric takes 1 hour. 
2.  Water bottle, sip water every 10 mins
Pumping strategy : 
1. Pumping interval - less than 3 hours
2. ⁠First 3 mins, keep in minimum mode. 
3. ⁠increase slightly and pump for 15 mins 
4. ⁠switch back to low for 3 mins before turning off
5. ⁠Pump 9-10 times a day
6. ⁠Do this for 3 days religiously. 
7. ⁠From 4th day minimum 8 oz per session 
8. ⁠Download pump log app
